Celebrating new endowed chairs

UCSC is proud to announce the creation of two brand-new endowed chairs: one in entrepreneurship, and one that will benefit the campus's groundbreaking Global Information Internship Program.

Narinder Singh Kapany, a former UCSC professor and currently a UC Santa Cruz Foundation Trustee, made a $500,000 gift to establish an endowed chair in entrepreneurship.

A pioneering fiber-optics researcher, and an entrepreneur in his own right, Kapany will help entrepreneurship programs grow and thrive through his generous support.

UCSC offers courses in business development and business planning, as well as seminars and other opportunities for students to meet with venture capitalists, business managers, and experienced entrepreneurs.

"They learn how to turn good ideas into viable business plans," said Brent Haddad, associate dean of engineering for technology management.

Kapany, who was a Regents Professor at UC Santa Cruz from 1977 to 1983, has given before—he endowed the Narinder Singh Kapany Chair in Optoelectronics at the Baskin School of Engineering in 1999.

Another respected member of the UCSC community, Global Information Internship Program advisory board member Mark Headley (Stevenson '83, politics and economics), recently made a gift to the program that will establish the Dorothy E. Everett Endowed Chair.

Paul Lubeck, executive director of GIIP, practiced "social entrepreneurship" by working with the donor and nurturing the gift through the foundation.

The endowment will support the management, staffing, instruction, and other needs of GIIP, a program that mentors students in information technology and social entrepreneurship to advance social justice, sustainable development, peace, and gender equity.

"We put so many resources and training into a class that helps students develop great projects," said Katie Roper, GIIP's managing director. "The endowment will ensure that GIIP will continue to be a valuable resource."

Even before his recent generous gift, Headley was a longstanding GIIP supporter. "I think taking students from Santa Cruz and placing them into some of the most economically and sociologically challenged places on the planet—and doing that safely and responsibly—is an amazing accomplishment," he said recently.
